Academic Motivation

My primary academic motivation has come from my positive exposure to science. During secondary and high school years, I had been strongly inspired by my academic role models and was having early chances to get started with research. In addition to that, my experiences studying at a research-intensive institution greatly contribute to my desire to move forward with academia. Being part of a large public campus, I have gone through golden opportunities to get involved in multiple working environments including both industrial-like jobs and research-based positions. Especially, while working as a teaching assistant in the field of computer science and a research team member of the Vertically Integrated Projects (VIP) Program, focusing on augmenting a teachable robot with adaptive cognitive and social support, I have recognized my fascination with computer science education. I am also interested in theoretical computer science, from which I have been actively engaged in the integration of learning theories and cognitive psychology principles with algorithm designs to optimize self-studying approaches.

I hope to contribute my experience to make computer science more accessible to all who want to join the field.

Diversity Background

I had been spending most of my life in Lao Cai, a small province of the mountainous Northwest region of Vietnam, bordering the province of Yunnan, China. Thanks to that, I had been absorbing a diverse set of cultures from Northwestern Vietnam and Southwestern China. With more than 20 of Vietnam’s ethnic minority groups, I would proudly call my hometown “a melting pot of cultures and history”. I had been traveling around to the rural areas in my province and volunteering in many Vietnam-China Cultural Festivals, so I am conversational in Hmong-Mien language and Chinese.

Further, I am sincerely grateful to be the first member of my family to branch out to the U.S for education. I ended up spending my university life in Tucson, which is located near the border with Mexico. Thanks to that, I one more time had a chance to get involved in a multi-cultural environment.

Research Experience

Throughout my undergraduate career, I have fortunately got chances to work on two projects of two different departments at the University of Arizona. That helps me to get familiar with the research environment and allows me to challenge myself with a variety of scientific areas, from which I would be able to identify my personal research interests.

Compositional System Lab Jan 2021 - Present

Dr. Jonathan Sprinkle is working on a multi-campus project on self-driving cars and connected vehicles, which aims to construct advanced models of vehicles suitable to the model-based design of cyber-physical systems and reduce traffic congestion effects by expertly controlling the velocity of vehicles that are driving in congested traffic. As an undergraduate research assistant in his Compositional Systems Lab, I collaborate with graduate students, Post-Docs and other members of the project in coding and computing tasks that are aligned with data analysis, machine learning, data curation, and automation of data gathering to support the project. Further tasks include developing prototypes in Python and re-implementing code in C/C++ for speed on either embedded computers or through minicomputers such as a Raspberry Pi.

UA Holodeck Project Sep 2020 - Present

As a research team member of the UA Holodeck, an NSF-funded research instrument envisioned as a software/hardware instrument incorporating visual, audio, and physical components and novel technologies to enhance social interactions, I work under a directed research course within the Vertically Integrated Projects (VIP) program, directed by Dr. Winslow Burleson. Being part of the VIP-TAG (Tangible Activities for Geometry) team, I assist faculty and graduate students with research and development issues. My focus is on the psychological curriculum design and algorithm design, where I study how to integrate the lessons designed for children with the positional tracking system of a teachable robot.
